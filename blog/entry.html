Zero Day Matters

Chris Cleveland September 9th 2015

There is little debate that phishing is one of the worst threats plaguing the Internet. In an all too common scenario, an end user receives an email whose author impersonates that user’s bank. The email requests the user to maneuver to a login page via a link and confirm their username, password, and perhaps other information. The webpage looks completely legitimate--everything from the layout, corporate logo and other distinctive features appear normal. Little does the end user know that on the other side of the page is actually a hacker stealing their information.

While many assume themselves too smart to fall for such tricks, the threat still costs businesses dearly. The Microsoft Computing Safety Index estimated private sector losses of $11 billion in 2013 due to phishing. One may then shrug and say that these victims are the elderly, the young, or some other tech-gullible demographic. But a recent Proofpoint study revealed that the largest increase in 2014 targets were overloaded and multi-tasking middle management employees. It is not only grandma consumers falling for the bait, it is overworked and information-saturated employees and managers.

Part of the problem is that a hacker’s job is just so easy. In one sense, phishing is a simple act of deception--something long predating the Internet and probably humans as well. But computers and the Internet have made this visual ruse much easier and more scalable. The visual copy-paste functions of computers removes much of the craft required of previous generations of counterfeiters. Another advantage an adversary has over her predecessors is the automation and limitless scale of emails she can use to engage victims. Language does a poor job of conveying the scope of this disparity. However, perhaps the greatest driver of today’s threat is the lowered risk and complete absence of deterrence for a hacker. Over the timescale of a typical attack, the Internet provides near total anonymity, with no reliable mechanisms for quickly and reliably detecting and tracing an attack.

Of all the factors inflaming the scope of the threat, this lack of detection and consequential anonymity is the most significant. Yet high quality phishing detection has evaded the scope of traditional security research. It is, after all, a messy human-factor issue, which, unlike software or network vulnerabilities, seems to lack a neat technological solution. Best practices usually include employee training and educational materials on websites to protect customers. Technical standards around phishing reflect complacency about safety, insisting that blacklisting and SSL encryption provide sufficient protection. Progress has been made with adoption of DMARC and SPF standards, but these too have flaws that fail to protect a large portion of victims.

One must be clear that, when properly understood, none of these measures provide reliable prevention. Blacklisting refers to flagging a known threat after it happens in order to protect future victims. While flagging known threats after an attack is a good damage control measure, it does nothing to protect the initial victims. SSL is a way to establish secure, or encrypted, communications between two online entities like, for example, a bank and a customer. This works great if a customer is in fact logging in to her actual account page, but it does nothing to prevent an imposter from imitating her bank and stealing her information. These are all sensible measures, but they must not be confused with much needed proactive protection.

Successful protection protects users before a threat is identified by authorities, which requires real-time detection. This has been difficult to achieve, since a real time detection framework must work reliably and fast if it is to protect end users without encumbering their web experience. The best attempts for real time detection--working ahead of blacklisting efforts--have relied on heuristic methods that apply machine learning to various kinds of web data. These might learn, for example, to suspect login pages appearing on sites with certain URL lexical features or low page-ranks. Defenses based on these heuristic approaches, however, will at best work for a temporary time before an adversary learns to adapt. More likely, even in the short term, they won’t work with the accuracy needed to serve a user’s security and browsing needs. Nonetheless, the principal issue remains that past data is not a good prediction of future data when an adversary is involved.

Only when phishing is approached from a first principles standpoint, rather than a heuristic one, can we build true proactive protection. A first principles approach is perhaps the topic of another post. In short, it means viewing the problem from the hacker’s standpoint, asking what must work for the attack to succeed, and then rigorously reverse engineering a defense that makes those success conditions impossible. Pixm’s technology is designed to precisely fulfill this approach and cripple an adversary's ability to steal sensitive online information. This is not reactive damage control, behavioural training, or heuristic based detection, but rather real time and reliable prevention.

The Internet indeed provides phishers both ease of counterfeit and scalability in their digital heist, but proper innovation and compliance measures can drive real-time protection and, ultimately, deterrence. Phishing is a threat that weakens online trust between brands and customers, employees and employers, and citizens and public institutions. It also targets third world individuals whose primary means of banking is via remote access as well as disaster victims who are deprived of dire resources via Red Cross and UNICEF impersonations. Pixm’s purpose is not to add another damage control half measure to a checklist. Our mission is to eradicate the threat with and agile and reliable real time protection.
